🐾 TIP

What to Consider When Traveling with Pets

RVing with dogs and cats is one of life's great joys — with the right preparation.

⚠️ Never leave pets unattended in an RV in hot weather, even with AC running. Generator or shore power failures can cause interior temperatures to spike to fatal levels within minutes.
Before You Leave

🏥 Vet Visit & Health Records

Schedule a vet visit before any extended trip. Get an up-to-date health certificate (required for crossing state lines and international borders). Ensure all vaccinations are current. Ask your vet about motion sickness medication if your pet is prone to car anxiety.

🔖 ID Tags & Microchip

Verify your pet's microchip is registered with current contact information. Add a travel tag with your cell phone number — not your home number. In unfamiliar territory, a lost pet may end up at a local shelter hours from home.

🏕 Research Pet Policies

Check campground pet policies before booking. Most allow pets but many have breed restrictions, weight limits, or leash requirements. National parks allow pets in campgrounds but often prohibit them on trails — research each park specifically.

On the Road

🚗 Safety During Travel

Pets should be secured during travel — either crated or in a crash-tested harness. An unsecured 60-pound dog becomes a 2,700-pound projectile in a 35 mph crash. Cats are most secure in a carrier. Never let pets ride in a tow vehicle bed or on laps while driving.

💧 Hydration & Breaks

Stop every 2-3 hours for water and bathroom breaks — more frequently in hot weather. Pack a collapsible water bowl and a gallon jug of fresh water. Many dogs won't drink unfamiliar-tasting water, so bringing water from home helps with first-day hydration.

🌡 Temperature Management

Monitor interior RV temperature constantly when pets are inside. Install a temperature alarm (several affordable WiFi-connected options exist) that alerts your phone if the temperature rises. At campgrounds, keep AC or fans running and check regularly if leaving pets briefly.

At the Campsite

🐕 Establishing Boundaries

Set up a portable pen or long tie-out line so your pet has outdoor space without wandering. Bring a familiar bed or blanket — routine items reduce anxiety in new environments. Let your pet explore the campsite on leash first before giving more freedom.

🦟 Wildlife & Parasite Prevention

Ensure your pet is on year-round flea, tick, and heartworm prevention — essential for camping. Check for ticks after every outdoor excursion. Keep pets away from wildlife food sources and never let them investigate animal dens or burrows.

🌿 Toxic Plants

Dogs and cats explore with their mouths. Many common campground plants are toxic to pets including wild mushrooms, azaleas, oak acorns, and various berries. Keep pets on leash in unfamiliar areas and watch what they sniff and mouth.

🎒 Pet Travel Pack Essentials

  • Health certificate & vaccination records
  • Medications (plus extra supply)
  • Food and treats (enough for the full trip)
  • Collapsible water bowl
  • Extra leash and collar
  • Waste bags (bring more than you think)
  • Familiar bedding or blanket
  • First aid kit (pet-specific)
  • Vet contact & nearest emergency vet info for each stop
  • Crate or crash-tested harness
🐾 Pro Tip: Download the Pet First Aid app from the American Red Cross before your trip. It includes step-by-step instructions for common pet emergencies and helps you find nearby emergency vets anywhere in the country.
