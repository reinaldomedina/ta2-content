🛞 TIP

What to Look for When Buying RV Tires

Tires are your only contact with the road. Don't cut corners here.

⚠️ RV tire blowouts are one of the leading causes of RV accidents and can cause tens of thousands in damage. Invest in quality tires and replace them on schedule regardless of tread depth.
Understanding the Basics

ST vs LT vs Commercial Tires

ST (Special Trailer) tires are designed specifically for trailers and have stiffer sidewalls to handle sway. LT (Light Truck) tires are used on motorhomes and tow vehicles. Commercial-grade tires (like Goodyear G-rated) offer the best load capacity for heavy Class A rigs.

⚠️ Never put passenger (P-rated) tires on a trailer — they're not designed for the load or heat buildup of towing.

Load Range & Ply Rating

Match or exceed the load range of your original tires. The load range (C, D, E, F, G) tells you the maximum load the tire can carry at its maximum inflation pressure. Calculate your actual per-axle weight and choose tires with margin to spare.

Rule: Never run at maximum load capacity. Aim for tires rated at least 10-15% above your actual loaded weight per tire.

Speed Rating

Most ST trailer tires are rated for 65 mph. If you travel at 70 mph regularly, look for tires with an 87 mph (L-rated) or higher speed rating. Running tires above their speed rating generates dangerous heat buildup.

What to Actually Check

DOT Age Code — The Most Overlooked Factor

Find the DOT code on the tire sidewall. The last 4 digits tell you the week and year of manufacture — "2419" means week 24 of 2019. Replace RV tires at 6 years regardless of tread depth. UV, ozone, and heat degrade rubber from the inside out.

✅ When buying used RVs, always check tire age. "Good tread" means nothing if the tires are 8 years old.

Sidewall Condition

Look for fine cracks (crazing or checking) on the sidewall. Minor surface cracking is normal but deep cracks or cracks around the tread grooves indicate the tire is deteriorating. Any bulge or blister is an immediate replacement — no exceptions.

Brand Reputation for RVs

Not all tire brands are equal for RV use. Brands with strong RV track records include Goodyear Endurance (ST), Maxxis, Sailun, and Michelin. Avoid off-brand Chinese tires (often marketed as "brand new" at suspiciously low prices) — they have poor heat resistance records.

Maintenance Tips

TPMS — Tire Pressure Monitoring System

A TPMS is one of the best investments you can make for RV safety. It alerts you to slow leaks before they become blowouts. Systems like TireMinder or PressurePro run $100-300 and can pay for themselves the first time they catch a problem.

💡 Always inflate RV tires to the pressure listed on the tire sidewall or manufacturer chart for your loaded weight — not the pressure stamped on the rim. Check pressure cold, before driving, every single morning of a trip.
